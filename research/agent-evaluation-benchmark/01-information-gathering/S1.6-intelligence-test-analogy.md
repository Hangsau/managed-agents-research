# S1.6 — Intelligence Test Analogy: AI Capability Benchmark Design Philosophy

## Overview
This document examines design philosophies from 9 major AI capability benchmarks, focusing on their definitions of "intelligence" and reasoning for task selection. These frameworks inform how to measure deployed system capability beyond isolated LLM performance.

---

### ARC-AGI (Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus for Artificial General Intelligence)

- **Source URL**: [Chollet (2019) "On the Measure of Intelligence"](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.01547); [ARC Prize](https://arcprize.org/arc-agi); [ARC-AGI-2 Technical Report](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.11831)
- **Stated definition of "intelligence" or "capability" it measures**: Intelligence as "skill-acquisition efficiency" relative to scope, generalization difficulty, priors, and experience. Chollet argues: "The intelligence of a system is a measure of its skill-acquisition efficiency over a scope of tasks, with respect to priors, experience, and generalization difficulty." Contrasts with narrow task performance; measures *learning efficiency* on novel problems.
- **Task design philosophy**: Core innate human priors only (object persistence, goal-directedness, basic counting, geometric/topological concepts). Deliberately avoids crystallized/domain knowledge to ensure AI and human start on equal footing. Tasks are "easy for humans, hard for AI"—testing generalization over memorized patterns.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: By restricting tasks to universal human cognitive priors, ARC ensures no unfair information advantage accrues to systems with task-specific training data. Success reflects learning capacity, not training corpus enrichment.
- **Contamination handling**: Private test set with unreleased problems; task authorship independent from public LLM training sources (hand-designed puzzles, not web-sourced QA pairs).
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **3.5** — Excellent foundation for reasoning/abstraction capability; however, deployed systems operate in rich environments with prior knowledge and memory—ARC's pedagogical isolation (stripping prior knowledge) reverses real constraints. Best for assessing *reasoning floor*, not integration capacity.
- **Key takeaway**: Measure what *cannot* be memorized—generalization efficiency on novel tasks with minimal domain context.

### BIG-Bench (Beyond the Imitation Game)

- **Source URL**: [Google BIG-bench GitHub](https://github.com/google/BIG-bench); [Srivastava et al. (2022) "Beyond the Imitation Game"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.04615); [Research publication](https://research.google/pubs/pub51569/)
- **Stated definition of "intelligence" or "capability" it measures**: Scaling-aware capability assessment. Recognizes LLMs show "both quantitative improvement and new qualitative capabilities with increasing scale." Designed to measure capabilities *beyond current model saturation* and to anticipate emergent abilities in larger models.
- **Task design philosophy**: 204 tasks from 450 authors across 132 institutions, spanning linguistics, math, science, reasoning, coding, social bias, and commonsense. Tasks intentionally target areas where scaling models are expected to excel or show novel behavior. Crowdsourced diversity ensures breadth over cherry-picked research publications.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Breadth across 204 diverse domains (not a single narrow construct) prevents gaming on one capability dimension. Collaborative design from 132 institutions distributes potential publication biases.
- **Contamination handling**: Tasks contributed post-GPT-3; not explicitly addressing training data leakage, but collaborative sourcing reduces likelihood of trivial benchmark overlap.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **4** — Excellent multi-dimensional coverage of reasoning, coding, social awareness, and emergence. Deployed systems face heterogeneous task mixes; BIG-Bench's domain breadth mirrors real-world capability demands better than single-skill benchmarks.
- **Key takeaway**: Measure emerging capability at scale across diverse domains, not saturation on existing tasks.

### MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding)

- **Source URL**: [HuggingFace MMLU dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/google/bigbench); [MMLU Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MMLU); [DataCamp explainer](https://www.datacamp.com/blog/what-is-mmlu)
- **Stated definition of "intelligence" or "capability" it measures**: Broad multi-domain knowledge recall and reasoning. Comprises 15,908 multiple-choice questions across 57 subjects (STEM, law, medicine, nutrition). Designed to test breadth of knowledge across human expertise domains; ground truth from domain experts (89.8% expert accuracy baseline).
- **Task design philosophy**: Exhaustive subject coverage (57 domains) rather than depth. Each domain represented at multiple difficulty levels. Multiple-choice format with single correct answer enables automated scoring.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Span across 57 unrelated domains ensures no single strategy solves all; diversity prevents overfitting to one knowledge area.
- **Contamination handling**: Known limitation: Manual analysis (2024) found 6.5% of questions contain errors (multiple correct answers, unclear phrasing, or wrong ground truth). Highlights reliance on crowdsourced curation.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **2.5** — Measures knowledge breadth, critical for deployed systems; however, heavily contaminated and focuses on recall over reasoning. Better treated as *prerequisite* capability (does the system know facts) rather than *capability indicator* (can it solve novel problems).
- **Key takeaway**: Measure knowledge breadth across diverse domains; accept that knowledge recall is inherently contaminated by training data and prone to errors.

### MMLU-Pro (Upgraded MMLU with Reasoning Focus)

- **Source URL**: [TIGER-AI-Lab GitHub](https://github.com/TIGER-AI-Lab/MMLU-Pro); [Arxiv 2406.01574](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.01574); [NeurIPS 2024 accepted](https://openreview.net/forum?id=y10DM6R2r3)
- **Stated definition of "intelligence" or "capability" it measures**: Advanced multi-task language understanding emphasizing reasoning over recall. Removes trivial/noisy questions; expands answer choices from 4 to 10 to reduce guessing (random accuracy: 25%→10%). Explicitly requires chain-of-thought reasoning (CoT) prompting; gap between direct answer and CoT can exceed 19%.
- **Task design philosophy**: Filters original MMLU to remove easy questions (>80% inter-LLM agreement); adds reasoning-intensive items from TheoremQA, SciBench. Consolidates 57 domains into 14 subject areas. Expands multiple-choice distractors from 4 to 10, forcing deeper discrimination.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: 24 prompt styles tested for robustness; MMLU-Pro shows 2% mean sensitivity to phrasing vs. MMLU's 4–5%. Broader answer pool reduces exploitation of surface-level keywords.
- **Contamination handling**: Reasoning-emphasis reduces memorization advantage; human-expert validation for new distractors mitigates synthetic answer quality issues.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **3.5** — Better than MMLU for reasoning-over-recall; deployed systems must adapt to phrasing variations and handle ambiguity. Robustness tests map directly to real deployment drift.
- **Key takeaway**: Measure reasoning, not recall; force systems to discriminate among plausible alternatives under phrasing variation.

### HumanEval (Code Generation Benchmark)

- **Source URL**: [OpenAI HumanEval](https://github.com/openai/human-eval); [Statsig perspective](https://www.statsig.com/perspectives/humaneval-code-benchmarks); [DataCamp tutorial](https://www.datacamp.com/tutorial/humaneval-benchmark-for-evaluating-llm-code-generation-capabilities)
- **Stated definition of "intelligence" or "capability" it measures**: Functional correctness in code generation. Rather than syntactic or semantic similarity to reference solutions, HumanEval measures whether generated code passes hidden unit tests. Intelligence = ability to map English specification → executable, correct Python code.
- **Task design philosophy**: 164 hand-authored Python functions with natural-language docstrings and hidden unit test suites. Avoids surface-matching metrics (e.g., BLEU score); focuses on *semantic correctness* (code runs and produces right results). Pass@k metric: generate k samples, pass if any passes all tests.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Unit tests define unambiguous correctness; natural language docstrings avoid language that hints at implementation. Problems span multiple solution approaches, so reference solution mismatch doesn't penalize correct alternatives.
- **Contamination handling**: Hidden test suite prevents gaming; hand-authored problems limit overlap with open-source code repositories.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **4.5** — Directly applicable to systems performing code synthesis, automation, or reasoning-through-code. Functional correctness is an objective, non-subjective measure; deployed agent execution often depends on code correctness.
- **Key takeaway**: Measure objective correctness (passes tests), not subjective similarity to reference; one correct answer often masks many valid solutions.

### MBPP (Mostly Basic Python Programming)

- **Source URL**: [Google Research MBPP](https://github.com/google-research/google-research/tree/master/mbpp); [Arxiv 2108.07732 "Program Synthesis with Large Language Models"](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2108.07732); [HuggingFace dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/Muennighoff/mbpp)
- **Stated definition of "intelligence" or "capability" it measures**: Program synthesis from natural language. Complements HumanEval by focusing on entry-level tasks (solvable by programmers with <2 years experience). Measures ability to understand requirement specification and generate correct implementation.
- **Task design philosophy**: 974 crowd-sourced Python problems with natural language descriptions and 3 automated test cases per problem. Broader scope than HumanEval (data structures, string manipulation, basic algorithms); lower floor (entry-level complexity). Emphasizes requirement interpretation over algorithmic insight.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Crowd-sourced from multiple contributors; subset hand-verified by authors. Diversity of problem domains prevents over-optimization to specific patterns.
- **Contamination handling**: Automated test-based correctness evaluation limits gaming; 974 problems provide statistical coverage.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **4** — Bridges gap between academic benchmarks and real-world automation tasks. Deployed systems often perform routine code synthesis (data pipelines, helper functions); MBPP's entry-level focus mirrors actual deployment needs better than HumanEval.
- **Key takeaway**: Measure practical program synthesis at realistic difficulty; test requirement comprehension alongside correctness.

### GPQA (Graduate-Level Google-Proof Q&A)

- **Source URL**: [Rein et al. (2023) "GPQA: A Graduate-Level Google-Proof Q&A Benchmark"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12022); [GitHub](https://github.com/idavidrein/gpqa); [OpenReview](https://openreview.net/forum?id=Ti67584b98)
- **Stated definition of "capability" or "intelligence" it measures**: Mastery-level domain expertise. 448 multiple-choice questions authored by domain experts (biology, physics, chemistry) where expert validators (PhDs) achieve 65% accuracy and non-expert humans with 30+ minutes web research achieve only 34%. Measures whether system approaches expert-level reasoning.
- **Task design philosophy**: All questions written from scratch by domain experts; serial validation by two independent expert validators; questions excluded unless both validators approve. Canary string checks (GPT reverse-lookups) confirm no trivial memorization. Graduate-level problems that demand integration of deep domain knowledge.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: High bar (dual expert validation) ensures questions test genuine mastery, not surface tricks. Scratch-written problems avoid bias from textbook republication.
- **Contamination handling**: Extensive: canary strings prevent textbook leakage; zero-shot evaluation (no few-shot examples) enforced for fairness; dual expert validation catches ambiguous questions.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **3** — Excellent for specialized domains (medical diagnosis, chemical synthesis, physics reasoning); less relevant for general-purpose deployed systems. However, *approach* (high-bar validation, contamination checks) is directly transferable to domain-specific system eval.
- **Key takeaway**: Validate questions rigorously to match claimed difficulty; contamination checks (canaries) prevent false positives from data leakage.

### GSM8K (Grade School Math 8K)

- **Source URL**: [OpenAI Grade-School Math](https://github.com/openai/grade-school-math); [HuggingFace dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/openai/gsm8k); [Surge AI blog](https://surgehq.ai/blog/how-we-built-it-openais-gsm8k-dataset-of-8500-math-problems)
- **Stated definition of "capability" it measures**: Multi-step mathematical reasoning. 8.5K linguistically diverse grade-school word problems requiring 2–8 steps of elementary arithmetic (+ − × ÷). A bright middle-school student should solve all; measures ability to decompose problems and chain reasoning steps.
- **Task design philosophy**: Crowd-sourced problem generation (Upwork + Surge AI); multi-step intermediate reasoning required; solutions annotated with step-by-step work. Deliberately pitched at K-12 level to avoid exploitation of advanced mathematics knowledge.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Linguistic diversity (crowd-sourced across writers) prevents gaming on specific phrasing patterns. Elementary arithmetic only—no calculus/advanced methods—ensures fairness across model architectures that may lack specialized math training.
- **Contamination handling**: Verification round: different workers re-solve problems without author bias; 1.7% inter-worker disagreement on final solutions; problems with disagreement discarded or repaired.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **3.5** — Multi-step reasoning is critical for deployed agents; however, GSM8K's *word problem* focus misses deployed systems' real bottlenecks (symbolic reasoning over structured data, handling ambiguous specifications, reasoning over code). Good diagnostic for *reasoning length*, weak for *reasoning robustness*.
- **Key takeaway**: Chain reasoning over multiple steps; verification of ground truth is essential to catch human error in crowdsourced tasks.

### HellaSwag (Commonsense Reasoning)

- **Source URL**: [Zellers et al. (2019) "HellaSwag: Can a Machine Really Finish Your Sentence?"](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.07830); [Official website](https://rowanzellers.com/hellaswag/); [Recent validity critique](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.07825v1)
- **Stated definition of "capability" it measures**: Commonsense understanding of physical situations and counterfactual reasoning. Tasks show video/image context with 4 possible continuations; humans achieve >95% accuracy; designed so SOTA models score <48%. Measures whether system understands not just statistical word correlation but *causal physical plausibility*.
- **Task design philosophy**: Adversarial generation of incorrect answers: deceptive distractors contain semantically relevant words/phrases but lead to physically impossible conclusions. Tests counterfactual reasoning: *recognizing what is statistically likely vs. physically possible*.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Diverse contexts (cooking, sports, repair, etc.) prevent single-strategy solutions; adversarial filtering ensures distractors exploit statistical biases that would fool surface-level models.
- **Contamination handling**: Adversarial filtering (generate plausible wrong answers, keep hardest) inherently creates harder benchmark. However, recent critique (2025) identifies construct validity issues: grammatical errors, typos, equally valid alternatives, and misleading prompts in ~10% of items.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **3** — Commonsense reasoning is crucial for deployed systems (robotics, planning, autonomous agents); however, benchmark itself has documented validity issues and model performance gaps are narrowing. Useful diagnostic for *failure modes* (what plausible-but-wrong reasoning does the system generate) rather than *capability floor*.
- **Key takeaway**: Test counterfactual reasoning, not statistical correlation; however, validation rigor must match difficulty claims (recent findings suggest HellaSwag's construct validity is weaker than originally argued).

### HELM (Holistic Evaluation of Language Models)

- **Source URL**: [Stanford CRFM HELM](https://crfm.stanford.edu/helm/); [Liang et al. (2022) "Holistic Evaluation of Language Models"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.09110); [GitHub framework](https://github.com/stanford-crfm/helm)
- **Stated definition of "capability" it measures**: Holistic, multi-metric assessment across diverse scenarios. Rather than single accuracy number, HELM measures 7 metrics (accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, toxicity, efficiency) on 42 scenarios including 16 core scenarios (QA, summarization, dialogue, code generation, etc.). Deliberately exposes trade-offs between metrics (e.g., accuracy vs. toxicity).
- **Task design philosophy**: Scenario-based: each scenario = use case (e.g., "open-ended generation," "fact-based QA"). Rather than asking "what is the best overall metric," HELM taxonomizes the space of possible metrics and scenarios, then densely evaluates 30 models under standardized conditions. Comprehensive logging of all prompts/outputs enables external re-analysis.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Multi-metric prevents over-optimization to single accuracy dimension. Standardized adaptation and prompt engineering across all models ensures comparability; public release of all outputs enables community audit.
- **Contamination handling**: 21 of 42 scenarios newly introduced (not in prior benchmarks), reducing contamination from prior benchmark saturation. Standardized prompting reduces prompt-engineering advantage for some models.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **5** — Directly applicable. Deployed systems must optimize across multiple objectives (accuracy, latency, fairness, safety); single-metric benchmarks hide this complexity. HELM's scenario taxonomy and multi-metric approach mirrors real deployment decision-making.
- **Key takeaway**: Move beyond single-metric benchmarking; measure trade-offs and expose what optimizations hide (fairness/bias/toxicity regression when chasing accuracy).

### Inspect-AI (Evaluation Framework and Design Principles)

- **Source URL**: [Inspect AI Official](https://inspect.aisi.org.uk/); [Hamel Husain's blog](https://hamel.dev/notes/llm/evals/inspect.html); [Hugging Face integration guide](https://huggingface.co/docs/inference-providers/guides/evaluation-inspect-ai); [BetterBench Stanford methodology](https://betterbench.stanford.edu/methodology.html)
- **Stated definition of "capability" it measures**: Framework-agnostic definition. Inspect is a *methodology* for benchmarking (not a specific benchmark). Designed to evaluate capabilities across coding, reasoning, agentic tasks, knowledge, behavior, and multi-modal understanding. Emphasis on composition, reproducibility, and explainability rather than a single "intelligence" construct.
- **Task design philosophy**: Modular: Dataset (inputs + targets), Solver (chained reasoning steps: generate, prompt engineering, multi-turn dialog, agent scaffold), Scorer (automated or human grading). Composition allows custom solvers/scorers packaged as reusable Python modules.
- **Fairness / generality argument**: Comprehensive logging of all intermediate steps (prompts, outputs, reasoning traces) enables external audit and reproduction. Scalable parallelism (30+ concurrent evals per node) allows dense evaluation across models.
- **Contamination handling**: Emphasis on logging and reproducibility enables post-hoc contamination analysis. Custom Scorer implementations can integrate web-search checks or factual verification.
- **Relevance to deployed-system assessment (1-5)**: **5** — Inspect is a *framework*, not a benchmark; directly applicable to building custom evaluations for deployed systems. Modular solver/scorer design mirrors real agentic reasoning (planning, tool use, reflection); logging enables debugging deployed system failures.
- **Key takeaway**: Benchmarking methodology matters as much as benchmark content; composition (modular solvers/scorers) enables custom eval for domain-specific systems; comprehensive logging is non-negotiable for reproducibility.

## Design Philosophy Patterns

### Common Themes Across Benchmarks

1. **Avoid Single-Metric Bottlenecks**: ARC (learning efficiency), HELM (7 metrics), MMLU-Pro (robustness across phrasing) all explicitly reject binary accuracy as sufficient. Deployed systems require trade-off visibility (speed vs. correctness, accuracy vs. fairness).

2. **Contamination Awareness**: GPQA's canary strings, GSM8K's verification rounds, HELM's new-scenario emphasis, and Inspect's logging all assume training data leakage is inevitable; the goal is *detecting and quantifying* it rather than preventing it.

3. **Adversarial / Robustness Design**: HellaSwag's adversarial filtering, MMLU-Pro's prompt-style variation, and Inspect's multi-turn dialog solver all test systems under adversarial conditions, not just static distributions.

4. **Task Breadth vs. Depth Trade-off**: BIG-Bench chooses breadth (204 tasks, 132 authors) while GPQA chooses depth (graduate-level validation); deployed systems benefit from knowing both breadth (which capabilities matter) and depth (expert-level performance in critical domains).

5. **Objective Correctness Over Subjective Similarity**: HumanEval and MBPP use test suites; GPQA uses expert validation; ARC uses generalization on novel tasks—all avoid subjective judgment when possible.

### Unique Contributions

- **ARC-AGI**: Only benchmark explicitly grounded in information-theoretic definition of intelligence; design discipline to strip unfair priors is unique.
- **HELM**: Only framework to systematically measure trade-offs (accuracy vs. toxicity, efficiency) in a single evaluation.
- **GPQA**: Highest validation bar (dual expert sign-off) and systematic contamination checks (canary strings).
- **Inspect-AI**: Composition and logging design treats benchmarking as a framework problem, not a task collection problem.

**Relevance to deployed-system assessment**: No single benchmark maps to real-world deployment constraints (latency, safety, adaptation to distribution shift, reasoning-over-observations). Most benchmarks measure pre-trained capability; deployed systems' actual bottleneck is often integration (tool use, state management, fallback reasoning). A deployed evaluation framework should combine ARC's learning-efficiency lens, HELM's multi-objective trade-offs, GPQA's validation rigor, and Inspect's logging/composition philosophy.
