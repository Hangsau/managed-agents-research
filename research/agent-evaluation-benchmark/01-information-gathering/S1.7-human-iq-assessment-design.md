# S1.7: Human Intelligence and Capability Assessment Design History

Information gathering on psychometric principles, assessment frameworks, and cognitive ability measurement methodologies that inform AI system capability evaluation design.

---

## Foundational Assessments

### Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale, Fifth Edition (WAIS-5)
**Authors:** Pearson Assessments (David Wechsler legacy, updated 2024)  
**Source URL:** [WAIS-5 Overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wechsler_Adult_Intelligence_Scale)

- **What it measures:** Five primary indices: Verbal Comprehension, Perceptual Reasoning, Working Memory, Processing Speed, and Fluid Reasoning (expanded from four-index structure in WAIS-IV)
- **Design principles used:** 
  - Multi-index factor structure aligned with CHC theory (Stratum II broad abilities)
  - Separate verbal and performance (non-verbal) subtests to measure distinct cognitive pathways
  - Progressive item difficulty within each subtest
  - 2023–24 U.S. Census-reflective normative sample
  - Core administration streamlined to 7 subtests (vs. WAIS-IV broader set) to reduce ceiling effects in time-pressed settings
- **Reliability target / typical values:** Internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha) = 0.92–0.98 for index scores; test-retest reliability = 0.88–0.93 for composite scores
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** Timed subtests (Processing Speed, Digit Span Sequencing) prevent memorization; clinician-administered format with standardized prompts eliminates script variation; answer sheets kept separate from examinee view
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 4 — Multi-index construct measurement and reliability targeting directly applicable; timing constraints may not transfer
- **Specific lesson for our project:** Hierarchical index organization (broad abilities containing specific subtests) mirrors how AI capability frameworks should nest micro-benchmarks under capability categories; multi-mode measurement (verbal + non-verbal) suggests testing through diverse modalities (text, vision, code, reasoning) rather than single-dimension scoring.

### Raven's Progressive Matrices (Standard, Colored, Advanced)
**Authors:** John C. Raven and Lionel Penrose (1936, published 1938; modern revisions ongoing)  
**Source URL:** [Raven's Progressive Matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raven%27s_Progressive_Matrices)

- **What it measures:** Eductive ability (extracting meaning from complexity); fluid intelligence (Gf in CHC); abstract reasoning and pattern recognition
- **Design principles used:**
  - Non-verbal format to minimize language/cultural interference
  - Strictly monotonic difficulty progression (each item harder than prior within a set)
  - Progressive item sets (3×3, 4×4, 6×6 matrices) designed so test-taker learns from experience across sets
  - Item Response Theory (IRT) foundation for item calibration; guessing and carelessness modeled via 4PL IRT (lower/upper asymptotes)
  - Multiple difficulty variants (Standard, Colored, Advanced) for population differentiation
- **Reliability target / typical values:** Test-retest reliability ≈ 0.80–0.85; internal consistency ≈ 0.85–0.90; IRT-derived item discrimination ranges 0.3–0.8
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** Non-verbal, purely visual-spatial format prevents linguistic shortcuts; pattern completion design makes memorization ineffective (variant matrices defeat rote answers); no verbal instruction contamination possible
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 5 — Non-verbal abstraction reasoning directly transferable to prompt-agnostic AI capability tests (visual reasoning, code pattern completion, abstract problem solving); IRT framework applies directly; progressive difficulty scaling model is directly adoptable
- **Specific lesson for our project:** Monotonic difficulty progression and IRT-based item calibration prevent clustering at score boundaries; separating "guessing asymptote" from "reasoning asymptote" in IRT model shows how to distinguish random success from genuine capability. Design assumes learning-during-test is valid; benchmark design should clarify whether within-benchmark learning is a confound or feature.

### Stanford-Binet Intelligence Scales (Fifth Edition, SB5)
**Authors:** Gale H. Roid (2003, revisions ongoing)  
**Source URL:** [Stanford-Binet Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford%E2%80%93Binet_Intelligence_Scales)

- **What it measures:** Five cognitive factors: Knowledge (Gc), Quantitative Reasoning (Gq), Visual-Spatial Processing (Gv), Working Memory (Gsm), Fluid Reasoning (Gf)
- **Design principles used:**
  - Factor-analytic derivation of five factors from item response patterns
  - Verbal and nonverbal routing (examinee routed to appropriate subtest based on prior performance, reducing test burden)
  - Weighted factor scores to normalize cross-factor comparison
  - CHC theory-aligned subtest mapping
  - Careful item selection for content validity and bias reduction (diverse professional judgment review, fairness assessment across populations)
- **Reliability target / typical values:** Internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha) > 0.95; test-retest reliability = 0.84–0.95 across age groups; composite score reliability = 0.95+
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** Routing prevents overexposure to easy items (reducing memorization opportunity); diverse item types (matrices, vocabulary, quantitative) prevent single-strategy gaming; extended g-loading validation shows items converge on unitary construct, reducing strategy-specific variance
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 4 — Five-factor approach and routing logic applicable; reliability targets (>0.95 composite) are ambitious for novel AI benchmarks; bias reduction methodology valuable for multi-population AI testing
- **Specific lesson for our project:** Routing (adaptive subtest assignment based on performance) shows how to economize long benchmark suites while maintaining construct coverage; weighted factor scoring shows how to handle constructs of varying measurement precision without conflating scales.

### Cattell–Horn–Carroll (CHC) Theory of Cognitive Abilities
**Authors:** Raymond B. Cattell, John L. Horn, John B. Carroll (theory synthesis early 1980s; ongoing elaboration)  
**Source URL:** [Cattell–Horn–Carroll Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cattell%E2%80%93Horn%E2%80%93Carroll_theory)

- **What it measures:** Hierarchical cognitive ability structure: Stratum III (general factor g) ← Stratum II (9–16 broad abilities including Gf, Gc, Gv, Gsm, Gs, Glr, Gp, Ga, Gkh) ← Stratum I (80+ narrow, specific abilities)
- **Design principles used:**
  - Hierarchical confirmatory factor analysis across thousands of IQ test data points and prior research
  - Three-stratum structure derived from both Cattell-Horn Gf-Gc model and Carroll's three-stratum hierarchy (syntheses at 1985 convergence meeting)
  - Operational mapping: modern IQ tests (WAIS-5, WISC-V, WJ-IV, SB5, DAS-II, KABC-II) align subtests to CHC factors post-hoc or in design phase
  - Domain-specific sub-scales within broad abilities (e.g., Gf contains sequential reasoning, inductive reasoning, quantitative reasoning)
- **Reliability target / typical values:** Internal consistency of broad-ability composites typically 0.85–0.92; narrower abilities 0.70–0.85
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** CHC is an analysis framework, not a test; anti-contamination enforced by individual tests using CHC (e.g., WAIS-5's visual-spatial isolation of Gv tasks). Hierarchical nesting itself prevents factor confusion (low-level ability variance attributed to correct stratum, not g).
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 5 — Hierarchical taxonomy directly applicable as capability taxonomy scaffold (System capability ← Broad capability dimensions ← Narrow task skills). Three-stratum structure shows how to separate general performance from domain-specific strength; broadly validated across human populations.
- **Specific lesson for our project:** CHC demonstrates that a single flat "IQ score" obscures distinct capability dimensions; hierarchical models (g → broad → narrow) allow diagnosing specific weaknesses without confounding them with general performance. Operational test alignment (WAIS-5 maps to CHC post-design) shows assessment design need not precede theory, but theory must validate the structure afterward.

### Raven's Advanced Progressive Matrices (APM)
**Authors:** John C. Raven (extensions by subsequent researchers)  
**Source URL:** [Raven's Progressive Matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raven%27s_Progressive_Matrices)

- **What it measures:** Fluid intelligence (Gf) in above-average-ability adolescents and adults; eductive reasoning in complex visual-spatial patterns
- **Design principles used:**
  - 48 items (vs. 60 in Standard), high difficulty clustering to prevent floor effects in gifted populations
  - Single set of matrices with monotonically increasing difficulty
  - IRT calibration with upper-asymptote (guessing probability) and item discrimination parameters
  - Timed and untimed versions to separate speed-of-processing from reasoning accuracy
- **Reliability target / typical values:** Test-retest reliability = 0.85–0.92 (untimed); 0.80–0.88 (timed); internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha) = 0.88–0.95
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** High item difficulty and visual-spatial abstractness prevent mechanical pattern-memorization; untimed variant isolates reasoning from processing-speed confounds
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 5 — Separation of timed vs. untimed administration shows how to isolate reasoning from latency constraints; high-difficulty clustering prevents ceiling effects in capable systems
- **Specific lesson for our project:** Timed/untimed split exposes a hidden confound in many AI benchmarks: does performance reflect reasoning or inference speed? Separate measurement allows diagnosis. High-difficulty ceiling avoidance is critical for AI systems (unlike humans, ceiling effects appear at 99th percentile or higher).

## Personality and Non-Cognitive Assessment

### Big Five Personality Assessment (NEO-PI-3 / NEO-PI-R)
**Authors:** Paul T. Costa Jr. and Robert R. McCrae (original model 1978, NEO-PI-R 1992, NEO-PI-3 2010)  
**Source URL:** [Revised NEO Personality Inventory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_NEO_Personality_Inventory)

- **What it measures:** Five orthogonal personality domains (Openness to Experience, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism); each with 6 facet subscales (30 facets total in full form)
- **Design principles used:**
  - Lexical approach: factor structure derived from personality descriptors in natural language, validated across cultures
  - Multiple form lengths (60, 120, 240 items) for precision-burden tradeoff
  - Dual-mode measurement: self-report and observer-report forms to capture perception variance and reduce self-presentation bias
  - Within-domain facet structure (6 facets/domain) shows domain is not unitary; allows fine-grained profiling
  - Item pools constructed for internal consistency within facets (Cronbach's α = 0.60–0.70 per facet)
- **Reliability target / typical values:** Domain-level internal consistency: 0.86–0.92 (Neuroticism highest, Agreeableness lowest); test-retest reliability (8 weeks) = 0.80–0.83; inter-rater reliability (self vs. observer) = 0.40–0.60
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** Self-presentation bias reduced via observer-report cross-check; facet subscales allow inconsistency detection (e.g., facet scores contradicting domain score suggests response bias or context-specificity). Large item pools permit shortened forms without structural change.
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 2 — Personality traits measure stable predispositions; AI system "personality" (risk tolerance, creativity drive) is configurable, not stable. Observer-report dual-mode concept useful (system self-report vs. external evaluation), but trait orthogonality assumption may not hold for AI (e.g., "openness" and "conscientiousness" may be correlated in systems).
- **Specific lesson for our project:** Facet-level subscales within broad domains demonstrate that a single "domain score" can mask heterogeneous underlying capabilities; AI assessment should similarly nest narrow task-skills under broad capability dimensions and report both levels. Self-report / observer-report split shows importance of testing system against external gold standards, not just asking system "how capable are you?"

## Organizational and Leadership Assessment

### Assessment Centers (DDI Model / OPM Federal Leadership Assessment Center)
**Authors:** Development Dimensions International (DDI); U.S. Office of Personnel Management (OPM)  
**Source URL:** [Assessment Centers - OPM.gov](https://www.opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/assessment-and-selection/other-assessment-methods/assessment-centers/) and [Assessment Centers - DDI](https://www.ddi.com/blog/assessment-centers)

- **What it measures:** Leadership competencies, situational judgment, decision-making under pressure, interpersonal effectiveness, communication, conflict resolution; observable behavioral indicators in simulated work contexts
- **Design principles used:**
  - Multiple work-sample simulations (in-basket exercises, role-plays, group discussions, presentations) designed from job-analysis-derived competency models
  - Behavioral observation framework: raters score observable actions against rubrics, not inferences
  - Multi-rater consensus scoring: 2–4 assessors independently rate each examinee on same competencies, then calibrate scores in panel meeting before final consensus (reduces halo effect and single-assessor bias)
  - Construct differentiation: each exercise targets different competencies; no single exercise measures all dimensions (forces measurement breadth)
  - Real-time feedback loop: candidate performance in Exercise 1 (in-basket) informs their entry challenge in Exercise 2 (simulation), creating authenticity
- **Reliability target / typical values:** Inter-rater reliability (Intraclass Correlation, multi-rater) = 0.70–0.85; internal consistency across exercises (Cronbach's α) = 0.75–0.90; predictive validity (correlation with subsequent leadership performance) = 0.40–0.65
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** Multiple independent raters prevent individual bias; behavioral observation rubric prevents interpretation drift; simulations inherently context-specific, defeating memorization; real-time interdependence between exercises prevents parallel test-taking strategy
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 4 — Multi-rater consensus principle valuable for avoiding benchmark observer bias; construct differentiation (separate exercises for different competencies) mirrors modular benchmark design; behavioral observation framework applicable to task-trace analysis in AI systems. OPM's "day-in-the-life" simulation mirrors "realistic scenario" benchmark design.
- **Specific lesson for our project:** Multi-rater consensus (independent scoring + calibration meeting) is a gold standard for reducing single-observer bias; AI benchmark validation could benefit from multiple independent evaluators scoring the same system performance. Construct-per-exercise mapping prevents conflation of unrelated abilities (e.g., communication ≠ decision quality). "Day-in-the-life" simulation design shows that capability is context-dependent; isolated task performance (like microbenchmarks) may not reflect integrated capability.

### Structured Behavioral Interview (STAR Method)
**Authors:** Various (STAR = Situation, Task, Action, Result; widely adopted across tech and public sector)  
**Source URL:** [Using the STAR method for your next behavioral interview](https://capd.mit.edu/resources/the-star-method-for-behavioral-interviews/)

- **What it measures:** Past behavior as predictor of future capability; evidence-based examples of specific competencies (problem-solving, teamwork, communication, handling failure)
- **Design principles used:**
  - Standardized question prompt ensures all candidates address same scenarios (reduces interviewer drift)
  - STAR framework forces narrative specificity: Situation (context) → Task (role/responsibility) → Action (what you did) → Result (outcome); specificity reduces confabulation and retrospective bias
  - Structured competency rubric: rater scores response against behavioral anchors (e.g., "Did candidate articulate clear decision criteria?" Y/N), not subjective impression
  - Multiple interviewers (competency panel) rate same candidate independently, then reach consensus; eliminates single-interviewer halo/contrast effects
  - Blind or counterbalanced interview order to reduce order effects
- **Reliability target / typical values:** When properly structured (standardized questions, rubric scoring, multi-rater): predictive validity for job performance = 0.55 (vs. 0.10 for unstructured interviews); inter-rater reliability = 0.65–0.75; test-retest reliability (candidate consistency across repeated interviews) = 0.70–0.80
- **Anti-cheating / anti-contamination strategy:** STAR format forces detail disclosure; prepared canned answers become obvious when interviewer probes for specific numbers, names, dates. Behavioral anchoring prevents "sounding confident" from inflating scores. Multiple raters catch rehearsed narratives that mislead a single interviewer.
- **Transferability to AI capability assessment (1-5):** 3 — STAR structure (asking for specific observable outputs rather than general competence claims) applicable to AI evaluation (ask system for specific reasoning steps, not confidence scores). Behavioral-anchor rubric translates to task-level pass/fail criteria. Multi-rater consensus principle is valuable. Limitation: AI systems cannot reference past experience; STAR's predictive validity depends on assumption that past behavior = future behavior, which does not hold for stateless AI systems.
- **Specific lesson for our project:** Structured question prompts + rubric scoring + multi-rater consensus = bias reduction formula; unstructured benchmarks (open-ended prompts, single human judge, no rubric) are known to correlate poorly with ground truth. For AI systems, "STAR-like" structured prompts should ask for intermediate reasoning steps (analogous to Situation/Task/Action triad) before final Result, exposing reasoning quality, not just output.

## Psychometric Concepts Glossary

**Construct validity:** The extent to which a test measures the theoretical construct (latent variable) it claims to measure, rather than confounding constructs or measurement error. Established via factor analysis, convergent-discriminant evidence, and nomological networks.

**Criterion validity:** The extent to which test scores predict or correlate with an external criterion (outcome measure), such as job performance or diagnostic status. Split into predictive validity (future criterion) and concurrent validity (present criterion).

**Test-retest reliability:** The correlation between scores from the same test administered to the same individuals at two time points, measuring consistency over time and stability of the construct.

**Internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha):** A measure of how uniformly items within a test correlate with one another, ranging 0–1. High α (≥0.80) suggests items tap a shared construct; low α suggests heterogeneous items or measurement error.

**Item Response Theory (IRT):** A probabilistic model relating test-taker ability (θ, theta) to probability of correct response on each item, parameterized by item difficulty (b), discrimination (a), and guessing/inattention asymptotes (c, d). Allows equating tests of different lengths and difficulty.

**Ceiling effect:** Cluster of scores at or near test maximum, preventing discrimination among high-performers and inflating apparent population mean. Violates normality assumption for statistical inference.

**Floor effect:** Cluster of scores at or near test minimum, preventing discrimination among low-performers and deflating apparent population mean. Both ceiling and floor effects bias variance and reliability estimates.

**Differential Item Functioning (DIF):** An item that shows different difficulty or discrimination across demographic groups (e.g., gender, ethnicity) at equivalent ability levels, indicating potential bias or cultural specificity of the item. Detected via IRT or logistic regression methods.

**Ecological validity:** The extent to which test performance predicts behavior in real-world (non-test) contexts. High ecological validity indicates the artificial test environment generalizes to natural settings.

## Patterns Worth Borrowing

### 1. Hierarchical construct architecture over flat scoring
All major frameworks (CHC theory, WAIS-5 indices, assessment center multi-exercise design) organize capabilities in nested hierarchies: g → broad abilities → narrow skills. Flat single-dimension benchmarks (e.g., "overall capability score") obscure diagnostic information and prevent identifying specific weaknesses. Hierarchical scoring allows both holistic comparison and root-cause analysis.

### 2. Multi-axis measurement prevents method variance collapse
WAIS-5 separates verbal from performance pathways; assessment centers use distinct exercises per competency. Single-modality tests (text-only, code-only) confound construct variance with method variance. Multimodal measurement (vision, code, reasoning, language) decorrelates capability from presentation format.

### 3. IRT-based difficulty calibration and asymptote modeling
Raven's Matrices and modern IQ tests use IRT to distinguish guessing probability (lower asymptote) from reasoning ability (slope). Many AI benchmarks do not model this: a system at random chance gets 0% credit, obscuring whether 50% accuracy reflects half-reasoning-half-guessing or full reasoning + no-guessing-benefit. IRT allows fine-grained ability profiling, especially in high-capability regimes.

### 4. Construct-specific anti-contamination via task design
Assessment centers use behavioral observation rubrics and work-sample authenticity to prevent candidates from using communication fluency or test-familiarity to inflate scores. AI benchmarks should similarly isolate the target construct: e.g., code-quality-only rubrics (not "sound confident") to prevent output-formatting gaming; intermediate-reasoning requirements to prevent memorization of final answers.

### 5. Multi-rater consensus as gold standard for bias reduction
Both assessment centers and structured interviews achieve 0.65–0.75 inter-rater reliability and superior predictive validity (vs. single raters) via independent scoring + calibration meetings. No current AI benchmark uses multi-rater consensus validation. Implementing this in AI evaluation (multiple independent expert evaluators score same system response, then reach consensus) would substantially improve construct validity.

### 6. Routing and adaptive measurement economize test burden while maintaining breadth
Stanford-Binet and WAIS-5 use performance on early items to route examinees to appropriate subtest difficulty, reducing administration time while preventing floor/ceiling clustering. Analogously, AI benchmarks could adaptively select task difficulty based on system performance trajectory, reducing total tokens spent while covering a wider difficulty range (important given token costs and latency concerns).
